Business Challenge


A global information consultancy (B2B offering SaaS) had multiple products providing bespoke analysis and interpretations to industry.

Should products be merged? If so, what does that look like to increase both cross-sell and up-sell

I came in as a last-ditch attempt after several false starts and was the only UX person.



Communicated business needs and agreed ways of working


Time was spent clarifying the business needs in a way that was operationally useful (i.e., we had specific targets that met the overall business aims). With these, I could create a workable plan to answer both key questions and provide strategic direction to the consultancy. Key to this were three questions (simplified for effective communications): Who is the audience, what needs to be changed and what will the subsequent design look like.

Outcomes: An awareness of competitors, their capabilities, strengths and weaknesses.



Content Inventory and analytics


What were competitors offering that was similar or different? After deriving a set of useful measuring criteria, I examined a set of almost 60 competitors against my client to get a high-level gauge of where they stood. This also fed into user research by giving me further background in the topic.

Below shows a sample of the competitor analysis underway. This was summarised in a report.

Outcomes: I had documented the company's data model.


User Research


I began with a series of interviews of existing customers. The aim was to understand how they used this company's information (goals, barriers, etc) and topics were guided by existing desk research (from the content inventory, analytics and competitor analysis). The outcomes were analysed for themes and guided a survey that was issued to all existing customers. There was a specific strategic decision not to approach non-customers. The interviews identified themes among customers and the survey put some strength and numbers on those themes. From this work, it was clear that there was an overwhelming appetite among customers to merge all products. The first question was answered.

Outcomes: A good understanding of users and a distinctly no patterns (lack of homogeneity) among them. I was also aware of goals, needs, pain-points and barriers.



Card sort


So let's merge them; but what does that look like? We had the content already noted so a card sort was organised. Optimal Sort was used because customers were unable to attend locally. Below shows a dendrogram with k-means cluster analysis, a video showing the clusters being examined interactively and a plot of a new information architecture / data model in a spreadsheet.

Outcomes: I got user data for the next phase of creating an information architecture.


New Information Architecture


The plot fed into a new sitemap (below) along with a navigation system for mobile devices. Because of resource constraints, we were unable to do tree testing but felt confident the IA was workable. Increased functionality was added and because of the lack of consistency among users, capabilities for personalisation and customisation were also added.


Outcomes: I gained a structure that was understandable and complementary to users' needs.


Page design - key page (article)


Key to the company was the design of the template for individual articles. This was "terminal-node" information that represented the most value to customers so extra work was required to ensure they met requirements.

I examined some competitors to illustrate how good documents work by mapping user needs onto some existing designs. Included below are screenshots from the BBC and Financial Times with comments. The final picture shows a suggested design for the client with comments justifying the design decisions which originated in user research.


I worked with a graphic designer who created the below page designs. After I discussed the user needs and justifications for design decisions, she created five different candidates for consideration using those requirements.

The question remained of how all these different products should hang together in a way that made sense to users.

Outcomes: I had the basic design of the key page for all users.


Prototype for demo and usability testing


After some creative thinking, I gained insight into how the information should be structured to be navigable and findable. The key was geographic location which was the one aspect mentioned by all users. There were multiple ways this could be defined: by region (e.g., continental), country, by province or by city.

This was hard to communicate to stakeholder. I created a clickable prototype using HTML/CSS/JS (using the Bootstrap framework) with some demonstration pages. This allowed a heuristic analysis, usability testing and demonstration to stakeholders. Below shows the initial page with additional functionality for users (including user-defined locations), a menu item, and information on a map.


Outcomes: I had a model to show how the new interface worked, how users would navigate and what additional functionality could be provided.
